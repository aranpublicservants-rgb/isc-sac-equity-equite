How to read what you see

  • Bar against a marker. The bar is ISC's actual representation. The gold marker is ISC's own departmental workforce availability. Short of the marker means below benchmark, and the text beside every bar says so in words, not only colour.
  • Gaps in people, not just points. A 3.9 point disability gap is 320 colleagues. Points get rounded in a briefing. People do not.
  • The label tells you the source. official transcribed from TBS, PSC or ISC. computed a gap or share worked from official figures. enter a value you supply. interpretation a hypothesis to test, never a finding.
  • "supp." means suppressed. Counts of 1 to 5 are withheld to protect privacy. Suppressed is not zero.

Three cautions, because a number out of context does harm

One · a flag is a place to look, not a verdict

A gap against ISC's own availability says investigate here. It does not say non-compliance. Workforce availability is a floor under the Employment Equity Act, not a ceiling, and TBS says as much.

Two · the count understates the problem where trust is lowest

Self-identification is voluntary. When people do not trust the workplace, they self-identify less, which thins the very numbers that would expose the worst units. Treat self-ID completion as an outcome, not a footnote.

Three · the most damning data is the data ISC will not find here

Executive representation by level, the First Nations, Inuit and Métis split, acting appointments, accommodation timelines. None of it is published at the department grain. The request generator exists for exactly that.

From a flag to an ask, in four steps

The tool is built to be used in this order. Each step has a home in one of the tabs.

Why ISC is held to its own bar

ISC's 2024-25 Departmental Results Report describes the department's mandate as confronting "historical and ongoing systemic racism and discrimination rooted in colonialism," and points to Joyce's Principle, its commitment against anti-Indigenous racism in health systems. A department that names systemic racism as the harm it exists to undo cannot treat the same patterns inside its own workforce as a culture problem to be managed. That reframing runs through this whole tool: racism and discrimination here are organizational hazards with legal definitions, not values to aspire to.

Indigenous workforce: presence is not the whole story

27.4 percent is real and worth defending. It is also one aggregate hiding the questions that matter for a department whose mandate is Indigenous self-determination: which nations are present, at what levels, and whether the people serving First Nations, Inuit and Métis communities reflect those communities at the table where decisions get made.

The split this page cannot show, and why it bites

TBS publishes Indigenous representation as one number per department. The breakdown into First Nations, Inuit, Métis and other Indigenous exists, but only public-service-wide in the snapshot, and department-by-subgroup inside the PSC dashboard. For a department serving distinct nations, an undifferentiated total can hide Inuit underrepresentation relative to the northern communities ISC serves directly, through nursing stations, Jordan's Principle and the Inuit Child First Initiative.

Pull ISC's First Nations, Inuit and Métis split

The PSC employment equity by organization dashboard reports employees, applicants, hires, promotions and acting appointments by Indigenous subgroup for each department. It is the only public source for ISC's subgroup distribution and for acting appointments, the rehearsal space for promotion that no annual table prints. The request generator writes the internal ask if you need it by level and tenure.

Disability and accommodation: where the gap and the experience agree

This is the one place ISC's representation shortfall and its employees' own words line up. Two independent sources, ISC's 2023-24 internal consultation and the interdepartmental Better Accommodation Project audit, describe the same thing: people with disabilities are under-recruited, and those already inside meet an accommodation process they call slow, bureaucratic and dehumanizing.

What the 2023-24 internal consultation found

  • 25.8% felt they had experienced attitudinal barriers at work; 17.0% had watched a co-worker experience them.
  • Accommodation outcomes over two years: 50% received it, 5.0% were refused, 37.5% were still waiting, 7.5% got a measure that was not the one they asked for.
  • 27.9% felt well supported by IT with accessible software; 8.2% did not feel supported and did not know whom to ask.
  • 84.1% found virtual meetings fully accessible, which leaves roughly one in six who did not.

ISC and IOGC 2023-2025 Accessibility Plan, internal employee consultation. official

What the Better Accommodation Project audit added

ISC joined the interdepartmental BAP and ran an external audit of its accommodation process. The findings tracked the internal consultation closely. Employees described a process built more around fiscal constraint than individual need, with delays sometimes lasting years, documentation demands that fall on the employee, and an experience they named as dehumanizing. Two Indigenous Talking Circles, facilitated by an Elder and opened by Deputy Minister Champions, surfaced the compounded barriers Indigenous employees with disabilities meet at the intersection of colonialism, ableism and a process never designed to be culturally safe.

ISC and IOGC 2026-2028 Accessibility Plan, issued December 2025. 256 employees with disabilities and 90 managers surveyed; subset interviewed; two Indigenous Talking Circles held. official

ISC's response, and the test of it

ISC is rebuilding its Duty to Accommodate process on the social model of disability, removing systemic barriers instead of demanding medical proof, with a "yes by default" posture for low-cost accommodations where the starting point is trust. The direction is right. The proof is a number, and it belongs in departmental reporting: time from request to implementation, tracked beside disability separations. If the redesign works, the delay shrinks and the retention gap narrows. If only the policy language changes, neither moves.

The disability triple bind interpretation

Under-recruitment (8.1% against 12.0%), an accommodation process employees call dehumanizing, and requests rising sharply from 2020 to 2024. Three failures compounding, not one. Fix hiring without fixing accommodation and retention and you fill a leaking bucket. Unearth it with: request-to-implementation time by year, against separations by disability status. Context behind the numbers

Three facts change how ISC's data should be read: it is a young department still forming its identity, it delivers mostly through partners and in the regions rather than the capital, and it only took control of its own HR in 2024.

A young department that names the harm

ISC was created by Order-in-Council in November 2017 and established by the Department of Indigenous Services Act on July 15, 2019, out of the dissolved Department of Indian Affairs and Northern Development. Its mandate is to support First Nations, Inuit and Métis in taking control of service delivery. Its own reports frame that work as confronting systemic racism and discrimination rooted in colonialism. For an employer, that self-description sets a higher bar: the internal workforce data has to stand up to the standard ISC applies to the systems it funds.

ISC Info Source; ISC 2024-25 Departmental Results Report. official

Regional and northern, not capital-centric

Most ISC services reach communities through transfer-payment agreements and direct delivery in the regions and the North: primary care and public-health nursing, Jordan's Principle, the Inuit Child First Initiative, status registration, environmental public health. That footprint is why ISC's departmental availability for Indigenous Peoples (5.7%) sits above the national line; the benchmark already reflects where ISC works. It is also why regional and northern roles, where many Indigenous employees sit, deserve their own cut rather than disappearing into a department average.

New HR, mid-restructure

  • 2024: ISC stood up its own HR service, ending the shared model with CIRNAC in place since 2019. Equity actions can now be targeted and measured against ISC's own reporting.
  • February 2025: a Sustainability Review Strategy began, to decide what is core to ISC's service-delivery mission.
  • September 2025: a realignment toward a one-ISC, single-window regional delivery model and an enhanced digital approach for individual clients.

Restructuring is the backdrop to every retention number in this tool. A separation during a realignment is not a voluntary exit, and the aggregate data does not tell them apart. Read separation figures in that light, and see barrier B7.

Sources, limits and accessibility

Every figure here is transcribed from a published Government of Canada source. Nothing is estimated, interpolated or modelled. Computed values are gaps and shares worked directly from official figures. Hypotheses carry the interpretation label.

Definitions you need

Workforce availability (WFA)The estimated share of a designated group in the Canadian workforce who could be recruited into the relevant jobs, from the 2021 Census and 2022 Canadian Survey on Disability. This tool uses ISC's departmental WFA, which reflects ISC's occupations and regions, not the public-service line. It is the legal benchmark under the Employment Equity Act, and TBS calls it a starting point, not a ceiling.
RepresentationThe share of employees who voluntarily self-identify with a group. Because self-ID is voluntary, true numbers may be higher than reported. Sex is taken from the pay system.
Expected number and gapExpected equals headcount times the group's ISC WFA. Gap is actual minus expected, shown in people and in points. A negative gap is a screening signal, not a finding.
SuppressionCounts of 1 to 5 are withheld to protect privacy, shown as "supp." A suppressed cell is not zero.
Indigenous subgroupsFirst Nations, Inuit and Métis, recognized under section 35(2) of the Constitution Act, 1982. ISC's subgroup split is published only in the PSC dashboard.

The limits of this tool, stated plainly

  • Date. ISC representation is the March 31, 2024 cycle, the most recent published at the departmental-plus-WFA grain. The 2024-25 report gives public-service totals; ISC's 2024-25 departmental row sits in the Demographic Snapshot Appendix Table 1, linked below.
  • No executive grain. ISC representation by EX level, and by Indigenous subgroup at the top, is not published per department. The most important number this tool cannot show, which is why barrier B4 and the generator exist.
  • Disability WFA carries a measurement effect. The 12.0% benchmark uses the broader 2017+ survey definition the current self-ID form does not match, so part of the 8.1% gap is measurement. TBS expects the modernized form to close some of it.
  • PSES is linked, not embedded. ISC's own results and demographic breakdowns are dashboard-served. This tool embeds the public-service benchmarks and deep-links ISC (org 17).
  • Separations include retirements and restructuring exits and the data does not separate them.

Accessibility of this page

Built to WCAG 2.1 AA. Status is carried in text and symbols, not colour alone. Tabs follow the ARIA tab pattern with arrow-key navigation; tables use row and column headers and captions; the generator and calculator announce their output to screen readers; motion respects the reduced-motion setting; contrast meets AA for text. This is an English working version. A French version is required for any Government of Canada publication, and the structure is built so the content can be paired one to one.
