The short version

  • Indigenous Peoples make up 27.4% of ISC employees, the highest share in the federal public service. Women are also above benchmark.
  • Two groups are below ISC's benchmark: persons with disabilities (8.1%, about 320 short) and racialized employees (20.1%, about 227 short).
  • ISC employees report more harassment (16% vs 12%) and discrimination (10% vs 9%) than the public service average, and less trust that they can use recourse without fear (46% vs 51%).
  • 23% of ISC employees say racism has harmed their mental health, compared with 16% across the public service.
  • The data ISC has not published, such as representation at the executive level and the First Nations, Inuit and Métis split, is named in the barrier register and can be requested with the generator.

How to read what you see

  • A bar with a marker. The bar is ISC's actual representation. The gold marker is ISC's benchmark (the share you would expect from ISC's jobs and regions). When the bar is short of the marker, the group is below benchmark. The text next to every bar says this in words, not just colour.
  • Gaps in people, not just points. A 3.9 point gap for disability is 320 colleagues. Points get rounded in a briefing. People do not.
  • The label tells you the source. official taken from TBS, PSC or ISC. computed a gap or share worked out from official figures. enter a value you supply. interpretation a question to test, not a finding.
  • "supp." means suppressed. Counts of 1 to 5 are held back to protect privacy. Suppressed does not mean zero.

Three things to keep in mind

A gap is a place to look, not a verdict

A gap against ISC's benchmark tells you to look closer. It does not prove non-compliance. Workforce availability is a floor under the Employment Equity Act, not a ceiling. TBS says the same.

The count can understate the problem

People choose whether to self-identify. Where trust is lower, fewer people share, which thins the numbers in the very units where the problem is worst. Treat the self-identification rate as a result worth tracking, not a footnote.

The most useful data is the data ISC has not published

Representation by executive level. The First Nations, Inuit and Métis split. Acting appointments. Time to provide an accommodation. None of it is public at the department level. The request generator exists for exactly that.

Why ISC is measured against its own words

ISC's 2024 to 2025 results report describes the department's job as confronting systemic racism and discrimination rooted in colonialism. It points to Joyce's Principle, ISC's commitment against anti-Indigenous racism in health care. A department that names systemic racism as the harm it exists to undo has to hold itself to the same standard inside its own offices. That is why this tool treats racism and discrimination as workplace hazards with legal definitions, not as matters of culture or good intentions.

Words used in this tool

Where the law and the data use older labels, this tool uses current, respectful terms in the writing and notes the data label in brackets. It uses "Indigenous Peoples" and, where the data allows, the distinct identities of First Nations, Inuit and Métis. It uses "racialized employees" (data label: members of visible minorities) and "persons with disabilities." "Designated groups" is the legal term from the Employment Equity Act for the four groups together.

Indigenous Peoples 27.4% · 21.7 points above

This is the highest Indigenous representation in the federal public service, and a real strength to protect as the department restructures. The open question is not whether Indigenous Peoples are present. It is whether they are present at every level, and across all three nations. One total cannot tell you whether First Nations, Inuit and Métis employees reach the executive ranks, or whether they sit mostly in entry and regional roles. ISC's own reconciliation evaluation reports targeted recruitment to raise Indigenous representation in leadership, which suggests the 27.4% does not hold all the way to the top. The annual table stops here. The PSC dashboard goes further.

TBS Indigenous Peoples representation by department, March 31, 2024: 2,232 of 8,148; expected 464; gap +1,768; 27.4% against a 5.7% benchmark. official

Persons with disabilities 8.1% · 3.9 points below

This is ISC's largest gap, and the one with the most evidence behind it. The shortfall of about 320 employees is not just a measurement issue. ISC's own consultations and an external audit describe under-recruitment together with an accommodation process that employees call dehumanizing. Representation and lived experience point the same way, which is why this gap deserves more weight than its size alone. See Disability & accommodation.

TBS Persons with disabilities representation by department, March 31, 2024: 657; expected 977; gap −320; 8.1% against a 12.0% benchmark. Part of the gap reflects the broader 2017 survey definition that the self-identification form does not yet match. official

Racialized employees 20.1% · 2.7 points below

This gap is easy to miss in a department that focuses, for good reason, on Indigenous employees. About 227 racialized employees are missing against the benchmark. ISC's 2026 to 2028 accessibility plan names racialized, Black and 2SLGBTQI+ employees as facing layered barriers, sharpest where they meet disability. The split inside the "visible minority" data label, for example the difference between South Asian and Black representation, is only in the PSC dashboard, never in the annual table.

Women 70.0% · 3.7 points above

Above benchmark, at 5,705 employees. The number worth following is not the total. It is how women are spread across job types and the executive ranks. Public service data shows women concentrated in administrative roles and thinner in technical and operational ones. ISC's own split by job type is not in this tool. Pull it from the snapshot tables when a specific decision turns on it.

Indigenous workforce: representation is not the whole story

27.4% is real and worth protecting. It is also one total that hides the questions that matter most for a department whose job is to support Indigenous self-determination. Which nations are present? At what levels? Do the people serving First Nations, Inuit and Métis communities reflect those communities where decisions get made?

The split this page cannot show, and why it matters

TBS publishes Indigenous representation as one number for each department. The split into First Nations, Inuit, Métis and other Indigenous identities exists, but only public-service-wide in the snapshot, and department-by-identity inside the PSC dashboard. For a department that serves distinct nations, one combined total can hide, for example, Inuit under-representation against the northern communities ISC serves directly through nursing stations, Jordan's Principle and the Inuit Child First Initiative.

Get ISC's First Nations, Inuit and Métis split

The PSC employment equity by organization dashboard reports employees, applicants, hires, promotions and acting appointments by Indigenous identity for each department. It is the only public source for ISC's split and for acting appointments, the step that often leads to promotion and that no annual table prints. The request generator writes the internal request if you need it by level and length of service.

The supports ISC already runs

ISC runs national Indigenous-inclusion supports that many departments rely on others to provide. These are places to point employees to, and bodies that can produce the detailed data this page is missing.

The question hiding in the total interpretation

A high overall share can hide a thin executive level and uneven representation across the three nations. Test it: get the split by identity and by level from the PSC dashboard, compare it with the communities ISC serves, and report the difference. If 27.4% drops sharply at the top, the barrier is advancement, not hiring.

Disability and accommodation

This is the one place where the representation gap and what employees say line up. Two separate sources, ISC's 2023 to 2024 internal consultation and an external accommodation audit, describe the same thing. Persons with disabilities are under-recruited, and those already at ISC meet an accommodation process they describe as slow and dehumanizing.

What the 2023 to 2024 consultation found

  • 25.8% said they had faced attitudinal barriers at work. 17.0% had seen a co-worker face them.
  • Accommodation results over two years: 50% received it, 5.0% were refused, 37.5% were still waiting, and 7.5% got something other than what they asked for.
  • 27.9% felt well supported by IT with accessible software. 8.2% did not feel supported and did not know who to ask.
  • 84.1% found virtual meetings fully accessible, which leaves about one in six who did not.

ISC and IOGC 2023 to 2025 Accessibility Plan, internal employee consultation. official

What the accommodation audit added

ISC joined an interdepartmental accommodation project and ran an external audit. The findings matched the internal consultation. Employees described a process built more around cost control than individual need, with delays that sometimes last years, paperwork that falls on the employee, and an experience they called dehumanizing. Two Indigenous Talking Circles, led by an Elder and opened by Deputy Minister Champions, showed the layered barriers Indigenous employees with disabilities face where colonialism, ableism and a process that was never built to be culturally safe all meet.

ISC and IOGC 2026 to 2028 Accessibility Plan, December 2025. 256 employees with disabilities and 90 managers surveyed; some interviewed; two Indigenous Talking Circles held. official

ISC's response, and how to test it

ISC is rebuilding its duty-to-accommodate process around the social model of disability. That means removing barriers rather than asking for medical proof, and saying yes by default to low-cost accommodations. The direction is right. The proof is a number, and it belongs in ISC's reporting: the time from request to the accommodation actually being in place, shown next to disability separations. If the redesign works, that time gets shorter and the representation gap narrows. If only the wording changes, neither moves.

Three problems at once interpretation

Under-recruitment (8.1% against 12.0%), an accommodation process employees call dehumanizing, and requests rising sharply from 2020 to 2024. Fixing hiring without fixing accommodation and retention will not close the gap. Test it: request the time from request to accommodation by year, against separations by disability status, in Request the data.

The pattern, in one line

ISC employees report more harassment and discrimination than the public service average, and less trust that they can raise a complaint safely or see it resolved. For a department whose mandate is to confront systemic racism, the survey says the work is not finished inside its own offices.

The racism climate, in employees' words

  • 23% of ISC employees agree racism has harmed their mental health, against 16% across the public service.
  • 71% feel safe to speak about racism without fear of reprisal, just below the public service (72%) and down from 77% in 2022 public-service-wide. Almost one in five do not feel safe.
  • 73% feel comfortable raising racism concerns with someone in authority, close to the public service (75%).
  • Among employees who experienced race-based discrimination, only about 1 in 9 are satisfied with how racism complaints are resolved, and 64% are dissatisfied.

2024 PSES, ISC (org 17), questions 24, 50, 51 and 72. Positive-answer totals. official

What is still missing, and worth pulling

These results are for ISC as a whole. The survey also splits results by group, which is the cut that shows whether Indigenous employees, racialized employees and persons with disabilities at ISC report worse experiences than their colleagues. That split is in the survey's data tools, not on the summary page. For a department where Indigenous employees are the largest group, it is the most useful query to run. Request it in Request the data, or open it in the PSES data tool.

Context behind the numbers

Three facts change how to read ISC's data. It is a young department still finding its shape. It delivers mostly through partners and in the regions, not the capital. And it took control of its own HR only in 2024.

A young department that names the harm

ISC was created by Order in Council in November 2017 and set up by the Department of Indigenous Services Act on July 15, 2019, from the former Department of Indian Affairs and Northern Development. Its job is to support First Nations, Inuit and Métis in taking control of services. Its own reports describe that work as confronting systemic racism and discrimination rooted in colonialism. For an employer, that sets a higher standard: the internal workforce data has to meet the test ISC applies to the systems it funds.

ISC Info Source; ISC 2024 to 2025 Departmental Results Report. official

Regional and northern, not capital-based

Most ISC services reach communities through funding agreements and direct delivery in the regions and the North: primary care and public-health nursing, Jordan's Principle, the Inuit Child First Initiative, status registration and environmental public health. That is why ISC's benchmark for Indigenous Peoples (5.7%) is higher than the national line. The benchmark already reflects where ISC works. It is also why regional and northern roles, where many Indigenous employees work, deserve their own view rather than disappearing into a department average.

New HR, in the middle of restructuring

  • 2024: ISC set up its own HR service, ending the shared model with CIRNAC that ran from 2019. Equity actions can now be aimed and measured against ISC's own data.
  • February 2025: a sustainability review began, to decide what is core to ISC's service-delivery job.
  • September 2025: a restructuring toward a single-window regional model and a stronger digital approach for individual clients.

Restructuring sits behind every retention number in this tool. A departure during restructuring is not a resignation, and the totals do not tell them apart. Read separation numbers with that in mind, and see barrier B8.

Sources, limits and accessibility

Every figure here is taken from a published Government of Canada source. Nothing is estimated or modelled. Computed values are gaps and shares worked out from official figures. Questions to test carry the interpretation label.

Words you need

Workforce availability (the benchmark)The estimated share of a designated group in the Canadian workforce who could be hired into the relevant jobs, from the 2021 Census and the 2022 Canadian Survey on Disability. This tool uses ISC's departmental benchmark, which reflects ISC's jobs and regions, not the public service line. It is the legal benchmark under the Employment Equity Act, and TBS calls it a floor, not a ceiling.
RepresentationThe share of employees who choose to self-identify with a group. Because self-identification is voluntary, the real share may be higher than reported. Sex comes from the pay system.
Expected number and gapExpected = headcount × the group's benchmark. Gap = actual minus expected, shown in people and in points. A gap below the benchmark is a signal, not a finding.
Positive answers (survey)The survey's total of favourable answers for a question, after setting aside "don't know" and "not applicable." For "victim of harassment" and "victim of discrimination," the figure shown is the share who answered yes.
Suppressed (supp.)Counts of 1 to 5 are held back to protect privacy. Suppressed does not mean zero.

The limits of this tool

  • Date. Representation is the March 31, 2024 cycle, the most recent published at the department level with a benchmark. The 2024 to 2025 report gives public service totals; ISC's 2024 to 2025 department row is in the Demographic Snapshot Appendix Table 1, linked below.
  • No executive view. ISC's representation by executive level, and by Indigenous identity at the top, is not published by department. See barrier B5 and the generator.
  • Disability benchmark includes a measurement effect. The 12.0% benchmark uses the broader 2017 survey definition that the current self-identification form does not match, so part of the gap is about measurement. TBS expects the updated form to close some of it.
  • Survey results are ISC-wide here. The split by group within ISC is in the survey's data tools, not on the summary page. Request it, or open the PSES data tool.
  • Separations include retirements and restructuring departures, and the totals do not separate them.
